Chemical structure and source

A Stable Ring, a Mobile Molecule

Atrazine's chloro-triazine structure explains both its herbicidal power and its persistence in aqueous environments.

Chemical Structure

Source: Agricultural Application

Atrazine is manufactured primarily by Syngenta AG and applied pre- and post-emergent on corn (Zea mays), sorghum, sugarcane, and Christmas tree farms. Application rates range from 1 to 4 lbs active ingredient per acre. In the United States, the Corn Belt states (Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, and Nebraska) account for the majority of national usage, with peak application occurring in April and May before the rainy season.

The chemical is formulated as a wettable powder, water-dispersible granule, or liquid suspension concentrate. After application, atrazine partitions between soil particles and soil pore water according to its Koc value. Because atrazine's soil adsorption coefficient is low relative to more lipophilic pesticides, a meaningful fraction remains in dissolved phase and is available for transport with rainfall runoff and percolating water. This mobility distinguishes atrazine from strongly sorbed compounds that remain near the soil surface.

Tile drainage systems (perforated pipes buried beneath agricultural fields to improve drainage) act as express pathways for dissolved atrazine. Studies using flow-weighted composite sampling have detected atrazine in tile drain effluent at concentrations exceeding 100 microg/L immediately following rainfall on recently treated fields (Thurman et al., 1991), orders of magnitude above the 3 microg/L EPA Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) for drinking water.

Chemical Reactions

Hydrolysis at C-6 (Cl Displacement)

C8H14ClN5 + H2O -> C8H15N5O + HCl
Atrazine + Water -> Hydroxyatrazine + Hydrochloric acid

Conditions

  • Catalyzed by H+ (acid) or OH- (base)
  • pH optimum: 2-3 (acidic) or greater than 9 (basic); negligibly slow at neutral pH
  • Rate constant (kh) at pH 7, 25 degrees C: approximately 1.5 x 10-8 s-1
  • Half-life at pH 7, 25 degrees C: approximately 540 days, derived from kh using t1/2 = ln2/kh
  • Temperature dependence: Q10 approximately 2.5, rate increases approximately 2.5-fold per 10 degrees C rise in temperature

The chlorine at position 6 of the triazine ring acts as a leaving group in a nucleophilic aromatic substitution (SNAr) reaction. The electron-withdrawing nitrogen atoms at positions 1, 3, and 5 activate the ring toward nucleophilic attack by water. The product, hydroxyatrazine (HA), retains the triazine ring and is itself environmentally persistent, though less mobile than atrazine.

N-Dealkylation: Two Parallel Metabolic Pathways from Atrazine

Pathway A, N-deethylation:
C8H14ClN5 + O2 + NADPH + H+ -> C6H10ClN5 + CH3CHO + NADP+ + H2O
Atrazine -> Deethylatrazine (DEA) + Acetaldehyde

Pathway B, N-deisopropylation:
C8H14ClN5 + O2 + NADPH + H+ -> C5H8ClN5 + (CH3)2CO + NADP+ + H2O
Atrazine -> Deisopropylatrazine (DIA) + Acetone

Conditions

  • Performed by soil bacteria (Pseudomonas sp., Rhodococcus sp., Nocardioides sp.) via cytochrome P450-type enzymes
  • Also occurs abiotically via UV photolysis (wavelength less than 340 nm) in surface water
  • DEA is the most commonly detected atrazine degradation product in US groundwater
  • DEA/atrazine molar ratio is used as an aquifer age indicator in hydrogeology, where a high ratio indicates older contamination

Oxidative N-dealkylation proceeds through hydroxylation of the alpha-carbon of the ethyl (Pathway A) or isopropyl (Pathway B) side chain, generating a carbinolamine intermediate that collapses to release the corresponding aldehyde or ketone. DEA retains the chloro-triazine core and exhibits similar aquatic toxicity to atrazine. Both pathways require molecular oxygen and NADPH as cofactors, provided by cytochrome P450 enzymes in microbial metabolism.

Inhibition of QB Electron Acceptor in Photosystem II

Atrazine + QB binding site (D1 protein) -> [Atrazine-D1 complex]
Blocked reaction (cannot occur):
Plastoquinone (PQ) + 2e- + 2H+ -> PQH2
Result: Electron transport chain halted -> NADPH not produced -> Calvin cycle fails -> cell death

Conditions

  • Occurs in all oxygenic photosynthesizers (algae, cyanobacteria, aquatic plants)
  • Effective concentration (EC50) for Selenastrum capricornutum (green alga): approximately 50-60 microg/L across studies (Fairchild et al., 1998)
  • Reversible at low concentrations; irreversible cell death above EC100
  • Competitive inhibitor: atrazine structurally mimics plastoquinone headgroup

Atrazine binds to the QB niche of the D1 reaction center protein, blocking the two-electron reduction of plastoquinone. This prevents re-oxidation of the primary electron acceptor QA, causing photoinhibition. Chlorophyll in excited state then generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), leading to oxidative damage to the thylakoid membrane, a process called photo-oxidative stress.
