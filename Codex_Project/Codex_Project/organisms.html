Three affected organisms

From Cells to Drinking Water

Atrazine stress begins at the photosynthetic base of aquatic food webs, then travels through amphibian endocrine systems and human exposure routes.

Three categories of living system bear disproportionate exposure to atrazine in aquatic and semi-aquatic environments: photosynthetic microalgae at the base of the food web, amphibians at the water-land interface, and humans at the apex of the contaminated food and water chain.

Organism 1

Phytoplankton and Green Algae

Selenastrum capricornutum, now reclassified as Raphidocelis subcapitata

Exposure Route

Atrazine enters surface waters via agricultural runoff and tile drainage. Phytoplankton, being fully aquatic photoautotrophs with direct water exposure, experience atrazine contamination immediately and at concentrations that can match or exceed laboratory EC50 values during spring pulse events.

Mechanism of Harm

As detailed in the chemistry section, atrazine competitively inhibits the QB binding site on the D1 protein of Photosystem II. At concentrations of approximately 50-60 microg/L (within the range documented in tile drain effluent), 50% of photosynthetic capacity can be lost (Fairchild et al., 1998). Below the photoautotrophic threshold, algal cells cannot produce sufficient ATP and NADPH to sustain growth, and populations collapse.

The ecological cascade is profound: phytoplankton are the foundational primary producers of aquatic food webs. Their suppression reduces dissolved oxygen production and starves zooplankton, larval fish, and filter feeders of their primary food source.

Adapted from Fairchild et al. (1998), Environ. Toxicol. Chem.

Human Health Link

Collapse of phytoplankton communities reduces ecosystem services including oxygen production and nutrient cycling. Bloom-bust dynamics driven by atrazine-mediated competition shifts can favor cyanobacteria (blue-green algae), some of which produce hepatotoxic microcystins that enter drinking water supplies.

Organism 2

African Clawed Frog

Xenopus laevis, with field evidence from Rana pipiens

The study of atrazine's endocrine-disrupting effects on amphibians represents one of the most debated and consequential chapters in environmental toxicology. Beginning in the late 1990s, Tyrone Hayes at the University of California, Berkeley, documented alarming reproductive abnormalities in laboratory-exposed Xenopus laevis frogs and in wild frog populations collected from atrazine-contaminated agricultural watersheds.

Key Findings

  1. Hayes et al. (2002) exposed male Xenopus laevis tadpoles to 0.1 ppb (microg/L) atrazine, a concentration one-thirtieth of the US EPA drinking water MCL of 3 microg/L, and found that a subset of exposed males developed hermaphroditic gonads (simultaneous presence of ovarian and testicular tissue). This feminization occurred at a concentration lower than what is routinely detected in Midwestern streams and drinking water systems during peak application season.
  2. A separate experiment within Hayes et al. (2002) exposed males to 25 ppb and documented a 10-fold reduction in plasma testosterone compared to controls. These are two distinct experimental results: intersex gonads at 0.1 ppb; hormonal suppression at 25 ppb. Both findings indicate atrazine disrupts the endocrine system across a wide concentration range.
  3. Field surveys corroborated the laboratory findings. Hayes et al. (2003) conducted lab and field studies in Rana pipiens (American Leopard Frog) and found that atrazine-contaminated sites showed higher incidence of gonadal abnormalities in wild populations. Sites with greater than 3 ppb showed elevated incidence of some reproductive pathology. These parallel findings in a second species, in both controlled laboratory and natural field conditions, substantially strengthened the body of evidence.

Aromatase Mechanism

The proposed mechanism: atrazine upregulates aromatase (CYP19A1), the enzyme that converts testosterone (C19H28O2) to estradiol (C18H24O2) by aromatizing the A-ring. The result is feminization, because the frog's own body converts male hormones to female ones.

Testosterone + 3O2 + 3NADPH + 3H+ -> Estradiol + 3NADP+ + 4H2O + CO2
The full aromatase mechanism requires three sequential P450-catalyzed oxidation steps at the C19 angular methyl group.

Population-Level Consequences

Feminization and hermaphroditism reduce reproductive success. Frogs with atrazine-induced intersex conditions cannot successfully fertilize eggs. Because amphibians occupy both aquatic (larval) and terrestrial (adult) niches, their decline signals broader ecosystem disruption. Monitoring data analyzed by Adams et al. (2013) found that North American amphibian occupancy declined at an average rate of 3.7% per year from 2002 to 2011.

Organism 3

Humans

Homo sapiens

Exposure Routes

Human exposure to atrazine occurs through three primary routes:

  1. Drinking water: atrazine is one of the most commonly detected pesticides in US municipal water systems; the EPA MCL is 3 microg/L, but seasonal spikes following spring application frequently exceed this in Midwestern utilities.
  2. Dietary exposure: residues on food crops and in surface runoff that contaminates irrigation water.
  3. Occupational exposure: farm workers and applicators via dermal absorption and inhalation of spray drift.

Health Effects

Endocrine Disruption

Like in amphibians, atrazine induces aromatase (CYP19A1) activity in human adrenal and gonadal cells. Studies of agricultural communities with high atrazine exposure have found associations with reduced testosterone levels in male residents of contaminated areas, intrauterine growth restriction in infants born to exposed mothers, and menstrual cycle irregularities in female farm workers (Sass & Colangelo, 2006).

Cancer Risk

The US National Toxicology Program lists atrazine as reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen based on sufficient evidence in animals. Subsequent epidemiological studies found elevated rates of non-Hodgkin's lymphoma in farmers with atrazine use and ovarian cancer in women in high-exposure Iowa counties.

Developmental Neurotoxicity

Gestational exposure in rodent models causes reduced brain weight, disrupted dopaminergic signaling, and spatial learning deficits in offspring. Human epidemiological evidence is emerging but currently limited.
