Overview

Why Atrazine Moves So Far

Atrazine (2-chloro-4-(ethylamino)-6-(isopropylamino)-1,3,5-triazine) is a broad-spectrum herbicide applied to over 60 million acres of US cropland annually, primarily on corn and sorghum. First registered in the United States in 1958, it became one of the most economically important agricultural chemicals in the world, and also one of the most consequential environmental contaminants in the water supply.

Unlike many pesticides that degrade rapidly in the environment, atrazine's triazine ring structure confers exceptional chemical stability. Its moderate water solubility (33 mg/L at 25 degrees C) and relatively low soil-adsorption coefficient (Koc approximately 100-175 mL/g) make it mobile in aqueous systems. Rainfall mobilizes surface-applied atrazine into tile drains, ditches, streams, and ultimately into the groundwater that supplies drinking water to millions.

The European Union banned atrazine in 2004 after detecting it persistently above drinking water thresholds. The United States continues to permit its use, though with restrictions. This divergence in regulatory philosophy (precautionary versus risk-based) sits at the heart of one of modern environmental chemistry's most important debates.

Likely judge questions

Q: "What is the mechanism by which atrazine causes aromatase induction?"
A: Atrazine activates the SF-1 (Steroidogenic Factor 1) transcription factor, which increases CYP19A1 gene expression. The exact molecular initiating event at the receptor level is not fully characterized. This is a legitimate scientific uncertainty worth acknowledging: strong enzyme-activity and in vivo data exist, while the precise initial binding event remains under investigation.

Q: "Why is atrazine still legal in the US if it causes these effects?"
A: The US regulatory standard requires demonstrated harm at realistic exposure levels through formal risk assessment. EPA disputes whether Hayes' dose-response model applies to human health risk at ambient concentrations. The EU applied the precautionary principle differently: any detection above 0.1 microg/L in groundwater triggers removal regardless of individual toxicity.

Q: "How does atrazine actually get into groundwater?"
A: Walk through the Kd calculation: Koc approximately 122 mL/g times foc 0.02 gives Kd of approximately 2.44 L/kg, a relatively low value that leaves atrazine mobile in pore water. Tile drains bypass the soil filtration zone entirely, delivering atrazine directly to streams. Losing stream reaches recharge shallow aquifers. The retardation factor of approximately 13 slows movement relative to advecting water but does not stop it over time.
